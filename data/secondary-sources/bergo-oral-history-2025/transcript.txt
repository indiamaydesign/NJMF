WHAT BERGO ADDS — HIS ACCOUNT, FIFTY YEARS ON
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Note on framing: this is one participant's recollection, recorded in
2025. It surfaces angles absent from the published secondary literature
(Ehn 1992, Bergh 2010), but it is one perspective among many — to be
weighed against the archival material, not used to overwrite it.

1. BERGO'S ORIGIN STORY: A 1970 LUNCH IN OSLO
   In Bergo's account, the consequential moment for his joining the
   project was an informal lunch in 1970. Hans Jørgen Raastad — leader
   of the Social Democratic Youth (AUF) at the end of the 1960s — told
   Kristen Nygaard about Bergo's initiative recruiting business-economy
   students from NHH (Bergen) to assist trade unions on the West Coast.
   Bergo says: "Hans invited Kristen and me to a lunch in 1970 and we
   agreed that I would join Kristen at the Norwegian Computing Center
   when I finished my studies at NHH and the University in 1971."

   This is not corroborated by any archival document — it lives only
   in Bergo's memory. Worth noting for the historiography: it is the
   kind of foundational moment that almost certainly happened in some
   form, but the exact framing ("we agreed I would join") is Bergo's
   reconstruction and may be more decisive in retrospect than it felt
   at the time.

2. THE BERGEN PRECURSOR: "SOSIALISTISKE SIVILØKONOMER"
   Before joining NR, Bergo was leader of a social-democratic student
   organisation on the West Coast. He had been recruiting NHH business-
   economy students into "Demokrati i hverdagen" (Everyday Democracy)
   campaign work, helping struggling West Coast trade unions whose
   industries were being hollowed out by competition from Portugal and
   Northern Ireland. After graduation, this informal network — which
   they called "Sosialistiske siviløkonomer" (Socialist Business
   Economists) — saw most members headhunted to Oslo. The network later
   "disintegrated" but several stayed in trade union work for life.

   Notable alumni from this Bergen network:
   - Hallvard Bakke → Minister of Culture
   - Svein Ragnar Kristensen → drafted "Arbeidsmiljøloven" judicially
   - Ole Berrefjord → staff of PM Gro Harlem Brundtland
   - Mats Sandman → Minister in Brundtland government
   - Alf Hildrum → CEO of Amedia, chief editor of TV1
   - Roar Flåthen (Kongsberg Våpenfabrikk trade union) → leader of LO
   - Jan Balstad → top LO leader, founded FAFO

3. NORSK REGNESENTRAL AS A "REBEL" INSTITUTION
   Bergo describes NR as "a rebel group of researchers, young and radical
   researchers, mainly mathematicians." Nygaard and his team had resigned
   en masse from the Norwegian Defence Research Institute (FFI) to found
   NR. Nygaard travelled to Washington and convinced Univac to donate
   "the largest computer in Europe at the time" to NR.

   NR funded itself primarily through customer-paid research; key early
   customers were Kongsberg Våpenfabrikk and Televerket. Simula —
   originally developed by Nygaard, Ole Johan Dahl and Graham Birtwhistle
   as an internal NR tool for designing telecommunication networks —
   became the conceptual foundation for object-oriented programming.

4. NYGAARD'S POLITICAL CROSSOVER
   When Bergo met Nygaard, Nygaard was "between parties" — formerly on
   the central board of Venstre (Liberals), having organised the election
   campaign of Aftenposten editor Aft­on Hegton (sic). Bergo convinced
   Nygaard to join Labour. Nygaard later became prominent in both Labour
   leadership and trade union leadership, but eventually left Labour
   too — described by Bergo as "such an innovative and rebellious mind,
   that he also did not fit into the Labour Party either."

5. THE MARXIST-LENINIST SHADOW
   Throughout the project, a Marxist-Leninist grouping was active on
   shop floors and in universities, and was succeeding in taking over
   some large local trade unions. Bergo says: "the establishment in the
   labor movement and the party, they suspected me of really being a
   hidden member of this communist movement. So I was not, I was not.
   But I talked to them and I had to work with them..." This explains
   the political register of the NJMF project's published documents
   (Marx and Engels references in IMG_2404 etc.) — the project sat
   inside an actively contested political-cultural space, not in a
   purely academic sociotechnical conversation.

6. THE EU 1972 REFERENDUM AS A POSSIBLE CAUSE OF FUNDING CUTOFF
   This is a NEW causal claim absent from secondary literature.

   Bergo: "The great controversial issue at the time, was the campaign
   for Norwegian membership in the EU. Kristen and I were working
   against Norwegian membership, and the leadership of the Labour Party
   and the Trade Union Corporation didn't like it. It was even worse
   when the Labour Government and the Norwegian elite lost at the
   referendum in September 1972. It's may be the reason we did not get
   funding for further research in Norway."

   September 1972 is also the month of IMG_0338 (Lange's "I would have
   liked to talk with Thorsrud") and IMG_0442 (Lange's private resigna-
   tion to Hansen) — the rupture of the AFI bridge happens in the same
   weeks as the EU referendum loss. Worth investigating whether the
   funding politics and the methodological dispute were entangled.

   Bergo: "In '74, we did not get any more friends, so they went to
   Denmark, and I went to my home." Nygaard left for Aarhus University;
   Bergo went back to journalism (Norwegian Broadcasting, 1975).

7. BRATTVÅG AND KONGSBERG AS THE TWO CORE FACTORY ENGAGEMENTS
   Bergo singles out two factory clubs as "really trade unions full of
   ideas and talents and it was pure joy to work with them": Hydraulik
   Brattvåg and Kongsberg Våpenfabrikk. The KVPOL system development
   at Kongsberg was a deep collaboration where the trade union convinced
   management to embed worker competence into the production control
   system: "It really was a win-win-process."

8. THE 100KG ATTIC: WHERE OUR PRIMARY DOCUMENTS COME FROM
   When the interviewer expressed interest in the project, Bergo wrote:
   "Det førte til at jeg har vært på loftet i dag, og hentet ned ca
   100 kg notater og rapporter fra Jern & Metall-prosjektet. Det var
   mer materiale der enn jeg husket, og det har ligget der fra vi
   flyttet til Bergen i 1984. Du er den første forskeren som har vært
   interessert!"

   Translation: "It led me to go up in the attic today and bring down
   about 100 kg of notes and reports from the Iron & Metal project.
   There was more material there than I remembered, and it has been
   there since we moved to Bergen in 1984. You are the first researcher
   who has been interested!"

   The 109 primary documents in our archive are a fraction of what
   Bergo has — and what he plans to deposit at Arbeiderbevegelsens
   Arkiv (Labour Movement Archive) once sorted.

9. BERGO ON WHY SWEDEN OFTEN GETS THE CREDIT
   Bergo's view on why secondary literature treats Sweden as the origin
   of participatory design (one perspective among several — Ehn 1992
   himself credits Norway as the lineage's origin, so this is not
   universal):

   "Forklaringen på det kan være at Kristen og jeg var omstridte
   forskere i Norge, og mange akademikere oppfattet oss som politiske
   aktivister, som vi også var. Men våre kritikere mente at da kunne
   vi ikke være akademiske forskere. I Sverige og Danmark ble vi ønsket
   velkommen både av fagbevegelsen og av universitetsmiljøene. Så der
   kan det ha kommet akademiske publikasjoner om deltakende design
   tidligere enn i Norge. Men det var i Norge metoden ble utviklet og
   iverksatt, og det var Kristen og jeg som dro til Stockholm, Gøteborg
   og Århus og fortalte dem om hva vi holdt på med."

   Translation: "The explanation may be that Kristen and I were
   contested researchers in Norway, and many academics regarded us as
   political activists, which we also were. But our critics meant that
   we could not be academic researchers. In Sweden and Denmark we were
   welcomed by both the trade union movement and the university
   environments. So there may have come academic publications on
   participatory design earlier than in Norway. But the method was
   developed and implemented in Norway, and Kristen and I were the
   ones who travelled to Stockholm, Gothenburg and Aarhus and told
   them about what we were doing."

   Three Swedish publications Bergo names from his attic boxes:
   - Pelle Ehn & Bo Göranson, "Perspektiv på Systemutvecklingsprosessen",
     SINFDOK, Stockholm 1974
   - Pelle Ehn & Åke Sandberg, "Fackföreningsrörelsen och forsknings-
     makten", DEMOS, Stockholm 1976
   - Åke Sandberg, "En ny företagsekonomi? Kunskap för demokrati i
     arbetslivet", LiberFörlag, Stockholm 1978

ANGLES BERGO'S ACCOUNT OPENS UP — TO BE WEIGHED, NOT ADOPTED
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The Bergo interview is one participant's reconstruction fifty years on.
It opens up two angles on the project that the published secondary
literature (Ehn 1992, Bergh 2010) does not foreground. These are
hypotheses to test against the archive, not corrections to the record.

BEFORE — Bergen as a possible precursor:
   Bergo positions the project's research method as something he had
   already been practising in Bergen with West Coast trade unions, and
   carried with him into NR via the 1970 Raastad lunch. If true, this
   would qualify the standard story that the "researchers studying
   workers" → "researchers supporting workers' own investigations"
   pivot was a methodological insight discovered inside the NJMF
   project. Worth investigating: the Bergen "Sosialistiske
   siviløkonomer" network does not appear in any NJMF document we
   currently have, so this is testable only by going outside our
   archive (e.g. asking Hallvard Bakke, Roar Flåthen, Jan Balstad).

AFTER — EU politics as a possible cause of the 1974 funding cutoff:
   Bergo claims his and Nygaard's leading role in the anti-EU campaign
   of 1972 cost them Labour Party and trade union goodwill, contributing
   to the funding non-renewal of 1974. The September 1972 EU referendum
   loss does coincide chronologically with Lange's resignation and the
   AFI rupture. Whether the political punishment hypothesis holds, or
   whether the 1974 cutoff has more conventional explanations
   (project completion, strategic shift toward agreements rather than
   research), is a research question — not a settled finding.

Both angles are valuable for the historiography because they shift
attention away from "the published Norwegian story" toward less-tracked
threads: a Bergen student network, and the way Cold War / EU politics
shaped what kinds of research could continue. Treat them as starting
points for further investigation, not as the new received version.
